On Friday, 20 March [vendor1] called and informed me that they were
finally able to get a quote for materials for repair of the sludge scraper /
chain system at the SID 128 WWTF in the amount of $182,000 for materials only,
excluding markups and field labor for install. This was significantly higher
than both [vendor1] and I expected. I anticipate the total repair price would be
$250k - $300k. This investment would get the SID to the point we were
previously, which was a facility that is unable to meet the ammonia effluent
limits (as required by our permit to operate). I don't think this repair is a
wise use of the SID's limited funds. Unfortunately, until the SID takes
decisive action, the WWTF will most likely be significantly out of compliance
with the permit effluent limits, which makes action urgent.

On Friday, 13 March I met [vendor2] at the WWTF. [Vendor2] is a sole
proprietor, wastewater engineer in the area that focuses on small scale
wastewater treatment solutions, mostly for agriculture and industrial
applications. [Vendor2] was confident that each of the Twilight Hills SID lots could
utilize an individual septic system. Some of the smaller lots would need an
engineered septic system ($20k-$25k) while the larger lots could use a
conventional septic ($12k - $15k). He also thought there would be some options
for a consolidated treatment facility on the land by the WWTF, though he
thought a combined septic in that location was unlikely to be feasible (in line
with Embris). The options for consolidated treatment would still discharge to
the creek and would therefore remain regulated by the State and EPA. He is
going to prepare a proposal for engineering services to better define the SID's
options for the SID board's consideration.

The options for the SID as I see them right now:

1) $250k - $300k (est) Repair WWTF sludge scraper system; WWTF remains out of
compliance and the SID may have to replace the facility to renew the NPDES
permit is early 2027.
2) $600k - $700k (est) Transition all of the SID houses to individual septic
systems; utilize [vendor2] to design septic systems for those lots that are too
small to utilize a conventional septic system. Demolish the existing WWTF.
This is the only option for which the SID no longer needs a WWTF Operator
and no longer has wastewater compliance requirements with the State and EPA.
The SID may be able to dissolve into the county once the construction debt is paid.
3) $TBD - Pay [vendor2] to design an alternate WWTF to be added to this list.
4) $1.5M (est) Replace the existing WWTF with a package WWTF plant.
This was Embris's proposed solution.

I think Option 2 or 3 would be best and I personally think Option 2 is the
better long term solution as it moves wastewater treatment from the SID's
responsibility to the responsibility of individual homeowners and is the least
costly option that doesn't leave the SID out-of-compliance. I can see the logic
of moving design forward on septic systems and the design of an alternate
consolidated treatment system. Do you have thoughts on another path forward?

### Background on chain scraper repair:

The chain scraper system is from the 1960's and is quite uncommon at this
point, as it has been phased out of use in most other commercial wastewater
applications. [Vendor1] received this $182k materials quote from
[vendor3] after trying to find a supplier since 10/31/2025.
[Vendor3] traveled to our
WWTF twice; the first was someone from Kansas City and the second was someone
from Kansas City and a national expert from Houston Texas.

Since October 2025, I've also been working with [vendor4] who
is a local wastewater contractor and they have been unable to find a supplier
having tried multiple of their industry contacts.
